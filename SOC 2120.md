### Definitions:
- **Population** - A complete group of individuals (people, animals, or things) that share a common characteristic, such as live in the same area or are a focus of a study
- **Cohort** - A group of individuals who share a common characteristic or experience, such as being born in the same year, starting a program together, being exposed to a particular event, etc...
- **Census** - Official count (or survey) of all members of a population
- **Survey** - Method of gathering information from a sample of individuals
- **Vital Statistics Registries** - Gov't systems (state/county level) that record life events like births, deaths, marriages, divorces, and adoptions 
	- Some countries track movement as well
- *Other population Registries* - Family bibles, church/parish records, graveyards... basically wherever people record major events

## Thomas Malthus & Impact on Population Theories
- Claims the world is a "population bomb" ready to explode
- Do famine, poverty, war, pestilence and environmental calamities exist because there are too many people in the world?
- Is population growth to be welcomed, even encouraged, because it will increase the pool of potential geniuses?
- Is population pressure the driving force behind invention and innovation, and thus a higher standard of living?
- Is childbearing haphazard or a rational response to immediate economic and social needs and demands?
- Can societies influence population growth without violating human dignity?
### Malthus' argument in a nutshell:
- His entire career focused on relationships between family size, poverty, and food supply
- Two postulates
	- Food is necessary to the existence of man
	- The passion between the sexes is necessary and inevitable
- Three laws
	- That population cannot increase without the means of subsistence is a proposition so evident that it needs no illustration
	- That population does invariably increase where there are the means of subsistence, the history of every people that have ever existed abundantly proves
	- The superior power of population cannot be checked without producing misery or vice

### Unfortunate relationship between population and food growth
- Unless unchecked, population growth increases geometrically
- Food growth increases arithmetically

### How to prevent population collapse (according to Malthus)
- Positive:
	- vice
	- misery
	- famine
	- pestilence
	- **causes of mortality**
- Preventative
	- Age at marriage
		- Only method Malthus endorses
	- **causes of fertility**
### The Neo-Malthusians
- People who generally buy the Malthusian "press of population" and "superior force of population over food" argument
- But also focus on the elements of "global calamity", "resource calamity", and "earth disaster" rather than the simple force of disproportionate mortality trimming the human population. Unsustainable use of natural resources
- Also differ because they think more can be done to restrict population growth than increase age at marriage
### Critical theories against Malthusian Logic
- Malthus became famous in his time because his ideas benefited the powerful and rich
- They wanted a logic, a story for why it's all right to 
	- exploit poor people, laborers, indigenous populations
	- Continue with global colonialism, slave trades, conquest of India, resource extraction from colonized areas
	- Turn profit from exploiting the colonized into investment in factories and industries
	- Grow capitalism and factories by exploiting cheapest labor possible - women and children
	- Privatizing land for the use of the wealthy and the total lack of control by the masses or poor
- **The results of this have not been great for society**

### Anti-Malthusians in a nutshell
- In almost every instance, the population movements that are now one of the most dramatic features of contemporary global life are the consequence not of too many people in developing countries, but of their being denied or deprived of secure access to fundamental productive resources.

        1.  **Crude Birth Rate: Number of live births per 1000 population in a given year: Calculated as (N/P) x K**
            
            1.  N = total births
                
            2.  P = total mid-year population
                
            3.  K = 1,000
                
            4.  Can easily be skewed by population composition
                
        2.  General Fertility Rate: Number of live births per 1000 women aged 15 to 49. Calculated with same equation, but different variables. Denominator is more exclusive, leading to less skewness.
            
            1.  Gives a super accurate snapshot and nothing else
                
        3.  Age-specific fertility: Number of births in a particular age group over women in that age group per thousand
            
            1.  Gives really good trend data
                
        4.  Total fertility rate: Synthetic measure (approximation of a snapshot).
            
            1.  Calculated as number of births per woman throughout her lifetime assuming she conforms to age-specific fertility rates.
                
            2.  Assumes age-specific fertility won't change
                
            3.  Assumes particular cohorts might be making different fertility choices
                
            4.  How to calculate: Break women into 5-year cohorts. List size of each cohort. List number of births by each cohort. Calculate age-specific rate by dividing column 2 by column 1.
                
            5.  TFR is 5 times the sum of all values of column 3.
                
        5.  Completed fertility:
            
            1.  The average number of children per women by cohort
                
            2.  Can only be calculated for cohorts over 50, but is totally accurate
                
        6.  Gross reproduction rate:
            
            1.  The average number of daughters that would be born to a woman during her lifetime if she passed through her childbearing years conforming to the age-specific fertility rates of a given year. Does a woman replace herself?
                
                1.  Called gross because it doesn't take mortality into account, and so is an overcount.
                    
            2.  Net reproduction rate: more sophisticated version of gross that accounts for mortality. Always lower than gross.
                
        7.  Completed fertility is a cohort measure; you will never get an accurate depiction of the current moment
            
        8.  Total fertility and crude birth are period measures; they will never give an accurate depiction of the future

## Review
**Total Fertility Rate:** - Avg. number of births per woman, found by taking total # of births / by total number of women for each period (5 year age group, for example). Sum these rates up, and multiply by 5.

**Period Measure:** - Measure of *many* cohorts at *one* point in time
**Cohort Measure:** - Measure of *one* cohort at *many* points in time

Cohort measures cannot be done until lots of time has passed, 50+ years usually

Demographic Dividend:
- Economic growth that a nation can undergo, as a population structure changes and a large number of working age people enter the labor force. This economic potential can only happen if:
	- Decent life-sustaining work is available
	- Education is widely available and gender equality in education is emphasized
	- Women's labor force participation is encouraged and supported
	- Health of the population is supported
	- The population has adequate nutrition, water, and safety
	- The society (and its population) is ready for new innovations and technologies
- Causes of this pivotal moment for the potential of a demographic dividend:
	- Fertility and mortality rates fall
	- families have fewer children
	- The children at the start of this transition enter the labor force as young adults (and are prepared for useful work)
